# Undergraduate Research Paper — Writing Guide

## How Undergraduate Papers Differ from Professional Ones

An undergraduate research paper follows the same general structure as a professional
research article (IMRaD) but expects a different level of assumed knowledge. The
audience includes the instructor and potentially other students, not just domain
experts. This means technical terms must be defined, methodological choices must be
explained (not just stated), and the Discussion must explicitly connect back to the
research question posed in the Introduction.

## Structure

1. **Abstract** — 150-300 words. Context, objective, methods, key results, conclusion.
   Keep it simple and clear.
2. **Introduction** — Background context with definitions of key terms. Clear research
   question or hypothesis stated explicitly. Brief overview of the approach taken.
3. **Methods** — Explain not just what was done but WHY each method was chosen. A
   professional paper might say "XRD was performed"; an undergraduate paper should
   explain what XRD measures and why it is appropriate for this question.
4. **Results** — Present findings with figures and tables. Describe what each figure
   shows before interpreting it. Avoid over-interpreting sparse data.
5. **Discussion** — Interpret results in the context of the Introduction's research
   question. Compare with published literature. Address limitations.
6. **Conclusion** — Summarize what was learned. Restate the research question and
   whether it was answered. Suggest future directions.

## Extra Explanation Expected

- Define all technical terms on first use, even common ones in the field.
- When citing methods, briefly explain the principle (e.g., "Scanning electron
  microscopy (SEM), which images surfaces by rastering a focused electron beam...").
- Connect every section back to the research question. The reader should always know
  why they are reading a particular paragraph.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Unexplained jargon.** Never assume the reader knows field-specific terminology.
- **Methods without rationale.** "We used technique X" is insufficient. Explain why X
  is the right tool for this question.
- **Discussion disconnected from Introduction.** The Discussion must explicitly
  reference the research question or hypothesis from the Introduction.
- **Over-claiming.** Undergraduate projects often have limited data. Acknowledge this.
  "Our results suggest..." is better than "We have proven..."
- **Copying structure from professional papers verbatim.** Professional papers omit
  explanations that undergraduate papers should include.

## Actionable Instructions for the LLM

- Define every technical term on first use, even if a professional paper would not.
- Methods section must include a "why this method" explanation for each technique.
- Discussion must open by restating the research question and then systematically
  address whether the results answer it.
- Use hedged language for conclusions from limited data ("suggests," "indicates,"
  "is consistent with") rather than definitive claims.
- Target a clear, pedagogical tone — the paper should teach as it reports.
- Keep the scope modest. An undergraduate paper should answer one focused question
  well rather than attempt to cover a broad topic superficially.
