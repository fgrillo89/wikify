# Photocatalysis fundamentals

Photocatalysis refers to the acceleration of a photoreaction in the presence of a catalyst. The classic photocatalyst material is titanium dioxide, which absorbs ultraviolet light to generate electron-hole pairs at its surface.

## History

In 1972 Akira Fujishima and Kenichi Honda demonstrated water splitting on a titanium dioxide electrode under ultraviolet illumination. This experiment is widely regarded as the foundation of modern photocatalysis research.

## Mechanisms

When a semiconductor photocatalyst absorbs a photon with energy greater than its band gap, an electron is promoted from the valence band to the conduction band, leaving a hole in the valence band. These charge carriers migrate to the catalyst surface where they participate in oxidation and reduction reactions with adsorbed species.

Surface defects on titanium dioxide nanoparticles act as trap sites that can either prolong the lifetime of charge carriers or facilitate their recombination. The balance between these processes governs the overall photocatalytic activity.
