# Photoelectrochemical water splitting

Photoelectrochemical water splitting uses semiconductor electrodes immersed in aqueous electrolyte to convert solar energy into hydrogen fuel. The semiconductor absorbs sunlight, separates photogenerated charge carriers, and drives the oxidation of water at one electrode and the reduction of protons at the other.

## Cell architectures

A photoelectrochemical cell may use a single photoactive electrode paired with a metal counter electrode, or two photoactive electrodes wired in tandem. Tandem architectures can absorb a wider range of the solar spectrum and reach higher solar-to-hydrogen efficiencies than single-junction cells.

## Materials challenges

Practical photoelectrodes must combine an appropriate band gap with stability in aqueous electrolyte under illumination. Titanium dioxide is stable but absorbs only ultraviolet light. Smaller-band-gap semiconductors such as hematite absorb visible light but suffer from short carrier diffusion lengths and surface recombination, which limit their photocurrent.
