# Biology Writing Guide

## Exemplary Papers
- Watson & Crick (1953) "Molecular Structure of Nucleic Acids" — Nature. One page that changed science. Economy of language at its finest; every sentence carries weight.
- Jinek et al. (2012) "A Programmable Dual-RNA-Guided DNA Endonuclease" — Science. CRISPR mechanism paper: logical flow from biochemistry to engineering applications.
- Doudna & Charpentier (2014) "The New Frontier of Genome Engineering with CRISPR-Cas9" — Science. Review that defined a field; clear diagrams of molecular mechanisms.
- Lander (2016) "The Heroes of CRISPR" — Cell. Historical perspective with narrative drive; shows how to frame a discovery story.
- Crick (1970) "Central Dogma of Molecular Biology" — Nature. Precise definitions that eliminated ambiguity; a model of conceptual clarity.
- Baltimore (1970) "Viral RNA-dependent DNA Polymerase" — Nature. Short, direct, data-driven. Let the experiments speak.
- Alberts et al. — "Molecular Biology of the Cell" textbook writing style: layered explanations building from simple to complex.

## Field Conventions
- **Structure**: Abstract, Introduction, Results, Discussion, Methods (often at end in high-impact journals). Supplementary figures/data are extensive.
- **Citations**: Numbered in Nature/Science/Cell. Author-year in many society journals (Genetics, PNAS).
- **Figures**: Gel images, fluorescence micrographs, flow cytometry plots, phylogenetic trees, pathway diagrams. Multi-panel figures with consistent labeling. Scale bars mandatory on all microscopy.
- **Tone**: Precise but accessible. Passive voice in Methods ("Cells were transfected..."), active voice in Results and Discussion. Hedging calibrated to evidence strength.

## Actionable Instructions
- Distinguish results from interpretation explicitly. "We observed X" (result) vs. "This suggests Y" (interpretation). Never conflate.
- Report sample sizes, statistical tests, and p-values for every comparison. State whether tests are one-tailed or two-tailed.
- Describe experimental organisms precisely: species, strain, sex, age, housing conditions. "C57BL/6J mice, 8-12 weeks, male" not "mice."
- Gene names in italics (e.g., *TP53*), protein names in roman (e.g., p53). Follow species-specific nomenclature conventions.
- Figures: include positive and negative controls in every experiment. Reviewers will reject without them.
- Methods must enable replication: concentrations, incubation times, catalog numbers for reagents, software versions for analysis.
- Use standard abbreviations after first definition. Do not assume the reader knows your subfield's jargon.
- For reviews: organize by concept or mechanism, not chronologically. Chronological reviews read like annotated bibliographies.
- Pathway and mechanism diagrams should flow left-to-right or top-to-bottom. Use consistent iconography for proteins, genes, small molecules.
