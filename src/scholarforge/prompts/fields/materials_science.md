# Materials Science Writing Guide

## Exemplary Papers
- Novoselov et al. (2004) "Electric Field Effect in Atomically Thin Carbon Films" — Science. Concise, direct reporting of a paradigm-shifting discovery. Clean data presentation, minimal speculation.
- Geim & Novoselov (2007) "The Rise of Graphene" — Nature Materials. Model review: historical context woven with technical depth, accessible to non-specialists.
- George (2010) "Atomic Layer Deposition: An Overview" — Chemical Reviews. Exhaustive yet readable; systematic organization by material class with clear mechanistic explanations.
- Goodenough & Park (2013) "The Li-Ion Rechargeable Battery" — JACS. Authoritative perspective combining electrochemistry fundamentals with practical device considerations.
- Chhowalla et al. (2013) "The Chemistry of Two-Dimensional Layered TMD Nanosheets" — Nature Chemistry. Strong visual storytelling through crystal structure diagrams and property maps.
- Snaith (2013) "Perovskites: The Emergence of a New Era" — J. Phys. Chem. Lett. Perspective that defined a subfield; clear framing of open questions.
- Ritchie (2011) "The Conflicts Between Strength and Toughness" — Nature Materials. Exemplary use of Ashby-style property maps to frame a fundamental trade-off.

## Field Conventions
- **Structure**: Introduction (application context + knowledge gap), Experimental/Methods, Results and Discussion (often combined), Conclusion. Supplementary Information carries detailed characterization.
- **Citations**: Numbered brackets [1], Vancouver style (ACS journals) or author-year (Elsevier). Cite primary sources for synthesis methods and characterization protocols.
- **Figures**: XRD patterns, SEM/TEM micrographs, XPS spectra, IV curves, Arrhenius plots. Always include scale bars, axis labels with units, and error bars. Multi-panel figures (a,b,c,d) are standard.
- **Tone**: Precise and quantitative. State deposition temperatures, pressures, thicknesses in exact numbers. Passive voice common in Methods ("Films were deposited..."), active elsewhere.

## Actionable Instructions
- Open with the application need or performance gap, not a history lecture. "High-k dielectrics require conformal coating of 3D structures" beats "In recent years, ALD has attracted attention."
- Report all process parameters: temperature, pressure, precursor, pulse/purge times, substrate preparation. Omission signals sloppy work.
- Present characterization data in logical order: structure (XRD, TEM) before composition (XPS, EDS) before properties (electrical, optical, mechanical).
- Use SI units consistently. Report film thickness in nm, temperature in celsius or kelvin (not both), pressure in Pa or Torr (pick one).
- Compare quantitatively against prior work: "Our films achieved a dielectric constant of 25, exceeding the value of 18 reported by Smith et al. [12]."
- Captions must be self-contained: state the material, technique, and key observation. "Cross-sectional TEM of 20 nm Al2O3 deposited at 200 C on Si, showing conformal coverage of 10:1 aspect ratio trenches."
- Discuss mechanisms, not just observations. Link processing conditions to microstructure to properties.
- Address reproducibility: report number of samples, standard deviations, and batch-to-batch variation where relevant.
