# Medicine / Clinical Writing Guide

## Exemplary Papers
- Framingham Heart Study papers (Kannel et al., 1961 onward) — Established the cohort study as a tool; clear reporting of risk factors with population-level data.
- Women's Health Initiative (Rossouw et al., 2002) — JAMA. Model of large-trial reporting: structured results, transparent adverse event reporting, measured conclusions.
- ACCORD Trial (2008) — NEJM. Complex multi-arm trial reported with clarity; frank discussion of unexpected findings.
- Ioannidis (2005) "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False" — PLoS Medicine. Provocative thesis argued with statistical rigor; changed how medicine thinks about evidence.
- RECOVERY Collaborative (2021) "Dexamethasone in Hospitalized Patients with Covid-19" — NEJM. Rapid, practice-changing trial reported with exemplary clarity under crisis conditions.
- Cochrane systematic reviews — Gold standard for evidence synthesis; structured, reproducible methods.
- CONSORT statement papers — Defined reporting standards; their own writing exemplifies transparency.

## Field Conventions
- **Structure**: Structured abstract (Background, Methods, Results, Conclusions), Introduction, Methods, Results, Discussion. CONSORT/STROBE/PRISMA checklists govern reporting.
- **Citations**: Vancouver numbered style [1,2,3] in most journals. JAMA and BMJ use numbered references.
- **Figures**: Kaplan-Meier survival curves, forest plots, CONSORT flow diagrams, ROC curves. Tables of baseline characteristics are mandatory for trials.
- **Tone**: Conservative and precise. Hedge appropriately: "associated with" not "caused." Patient-first language when relevant.

## Actionable Instructions
- Follow the relevant reporting guideline (CONSORT for RCTs, STROBE for observational, PRISMA for systematic reviews). This is non-negotiable.
- Report the primary endpoint first, then secondary endpoints. Never bury the primary outcome.
- Table 1 must show baseline characteristics by group with appropriate statistical comparisons.
- State the intention-to-treat principle explicitly. Report per-protocol analyses as secondary.
- Kaplan-Meier curves: include number-at-risk tables below the x-axis. Report hazard ratios with 95% confidence intervals.
- Forest plots: include heterogeneity statistics (I-squared, Q statistic). Weight each study visually by marker size.
- Adverse events: report systematically, not selectively. Use MedDRA terms where applicable.
- Discussion must address: comparison with prior evidence, biological plausibility, study limitations (at least one full paragraph), and implications for practice.
- Never overstate clinical implications. "These findings suggest X warrants further investigation" not "clinicians should immediately change practice."
- Register the trial (e.g., ClinicalTrials.gov) and state the registration number. Report deviations from the registered protocol.
