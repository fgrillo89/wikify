# Social Sciences Writing Guide

## Exemplary Papers
- Kahneman & Tversky (1979) "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision Under Risk" — Econometrica. Most cited paper in economics; accessible examples make abstract theory concrete.
- Tversky & Kahneman (1974) "Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases" — Science. Intuitive experimental vignettes that readers experience as participants. Writing as persuasion.
- Milgram (1963) "Behavioral Study of Obedience" — J. Abnormal and Social Psychology. Vivid procedural description; the reader feels the experimental situation.
- Granovetter (1973) "The Strength of Weak Ties" — AJS. Simple, powerful thesis developed through clear logical argument. Model of theoretical sociology.
- Akerlof (1970) "The Market for Lemons" — QJE. Used a concrete market example to explain information asymmetry. Accessible to non-economists.
- Ostrom (1990) "Governing the Commons" — Framework for institutional analysis. Clear typologies and design principles.
- Piketty & Saez (2003) "Income Inequality in the United States, 1913-1998" — QJE. Data-driven argument with clear time-series visualizations.
- Bandura (1977) "Self-Efficacy: Toward a Unifying Theory" — Psychological Review. Built a framework from empirical evidence; clear definitions that enabled decades of measurement.

## Field Conventions
- **Structure**: Introduction (research question + theoretical framework), Literature Review, Hypotheses, Methods (participants, measures, procedure), Results, Discussion. APA format dominates psychology and education.
- **Citations**: Author-year (APA style) in psychology, sociology, political science. Footnote citations in economics, law, history.
- **Figures**: Bar charts with error bars, scatterplots with regression lines, path diagrams (SEM), tables of regression coefficients. Effect sizes reported alongside p-values.
- **Tone**: Varies by discipline. Psychology: measured, empirical. Economics: assertive, model-driven. Sociology: interpretive, contextual. Always define constructs precisely.

## Actionable Instructions
- State the research question in the first paragraph. Social science papers that delay the question until page 3 lose readers.
- Operationalize every construct. "Social capital" means nothing until you state how you measured it (survey items, network density, etc.).
- Report effect sizes (Cohen's d, eta-squared, odds ratios), not just p-values. Statistical significance without practical significance is empty.
- For experiments: describe the cover story, manipulation, and dependent measures in enough detail to replicate. Include the exact wording of key stimuli or survey items.
- Regression tables: report coefficients, standard errors, confidence intervals, and model fit statistics. Label all variables with plain-language names, not variable codes.
- Address endogeneity, confounds, and selection bias explicitly. Acknowledge what your design cannot identify.
- Literature review should build an argument, not summarize papers sequentially. "Smith (2010) found X. Jones (2012) found Y" is a list, not a review.
- Use concrete examples and vignettes to illustrate abstract concepts. Kahneman and Tversky's success came from examples readers could feel.
- Pre-register hypotheses and analysis plans where possible. Distinguish confirmatory from exploratory analyses.
- For qualitative research: describe coding procedures, inter-rater reliability, and provide representative quotations.
