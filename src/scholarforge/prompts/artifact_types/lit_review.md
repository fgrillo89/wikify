# Literature Review — Writing Guide

## Exemplary Models

The best literature reviews synthesize rather than summarize. Refer to PMC3715443
("Ten Simple Rules for Writing a Literature Review") as a structural model. Annual
Review of [field] journals consistently demonstrate how to organize large bodies of
evidence into coherent thematic narratives.

## Structure

A literature review does **not** follow IMRaD. Use:

1. **Introduction** — Funnel from broad field to specific gap. State why this review
   is needed now. End with an explicit scope statement.
2. **Thematic body sections** — Organized by concepts/themes, never chronologically or
   paper-by-paper. Each section addresses a research question or ongoing debate.
3. **Conclusion** — Synthesize what the body of evidence collectively tells us. State
   2-3 concrete open questions or recommended next experiments.

## Key Conventions

- **Synthesize, don't summarize.** Never describe papers one-by-one. Group findings by
  theme, compare across studies, identify agreements and contradictions. A review that
  merely lists "Study A found X, Study B found Y" is a bibliography, not a review.
- **Critical assessment** is mandatory. For each major finding, evaluate: How robust is
  the evidence? Do methods differ across studies? Are results reproducible?
- **Citation density** should be high (1-3 per claim). Weave citations into narrative.
  Use author names for key findings, numbers for background consensus.
- **Scope discipline**: Stay focused. Discuss wider implications briefly but do not
  attempt to cover adjacent fields in depth.

## Actionable Instructions for the LLM

- Within each body section: state current understanding, present supporting evidence
  from multiple papers, note contradictions or methodological differences, identify
  what remains unresolved.
- The Conclusion must NOT restate the Abstract. It must go beyond summary to provide
  a forward-looking synthesis.
- Body section headings must be thematic (e.g., "Role of Surface Chemistry in ALD
  Nucleation") not generic ("Literature Review Part 1").
- When evidence conflicts, present both sides and explain possible reasons for
  disagreement (sample differences, methodological choices, etc.).
