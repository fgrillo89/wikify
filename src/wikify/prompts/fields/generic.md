# Generic Academic Writing Guide

Use this guide when the field cannot be determined or the work spans multiple disciplines.

## Cross-Field Exemplars
- Strunk & White "The Elements of Style" principles applied to academic prose.
- Orwell's six rules: never use a long word where a short one will do; cut any word you can; prefer active voice; avoid jargon when a plain word exists.
- Williams & Bizup "Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace" — characters as subjects, actions as verbs.

## Universal Conventions
- **Structure**: Abstract, Introduction, Body (field-dependent), Conclusion. Every section earns its place; if a heading has only one paragraph, merge it.
- **Citations**: Follow the target journal exactly. When unspecified, use author-year for readability.
- **Figures**: Self-contained captions, readable at 50% zoom, axis labels with units, legends that distinguish all series.
- **Tone**: Confident peer-to-peer. Not grandiose, not self-deprecating. Let evidence carry authority.

## Actionable Instructions

### Opening
- First paragraph: what is the problem, why does it matter, what is unknown. No historical preambles.
- Last paragraph of introduction: state your contribution in one or two sentences. The reader must know what to expect.

### Argumentation
- Every claim needs support: your data, a citation, or a logical derivation.
- Frame contributions using "They Say / I Say" structure: what is established, what is missing, what you provide.
- Distinguish observation from interpretation. Calibrate hedging to evidence strength; uniform hedging signals machine text.

### Sentence Level
- Active voice by default. Passive only when the actor is irrelevant.
- Characters as subjects, actions as verbs. "We measured" not "measurements were performed."
- Kill nominalizations: "analyze" not "perform an analysis of."
- Vary sentence length: mix 8-12 word sentences with 20-30 word sentences. Short sentences deliver emphasis.
- Be specific: numbers, not vague quantifiers.

### Paragraph Level
- One point per paragraph. Topic sentence first.
- Known-new contract: begin with familiar information, end with the new.
- Transitions emerge from logic, not filler words. If the link is clear, omit "furthermore."

### Figures and Tables
- Reference at point of need. Describe what it shows, then what it means.
- Captions must be self-contained: a reader skimming figures should understand the story.
- Tables supplement, never duplicate, the text.

### Common Pitfalls
- Do not start with "In recent years" or "Since the dawn of."
- Do not use em-dashes as parenthetical separators.
- Do not write meta-commentary: "This section discusses..." Just begin.
- Do not use bullet points in paper body sections (Methods lists excepted).
- Avoid elegant variation: keep terminology consistent throughout.

### Self-Revision Checklist
1. Does every paragraph have a clear topic sentence?
2. Can any sentence be deleted without breaking the argument?
3. Are all claims supported?
4. Are figures referenced at the point of need?
5. Does the conclusion add synthesis, not repetition?
